Strategy for the Sustainable Management of Mediterranean Forests

Programme 2021 - 2027 Interreg VI-B EURO Mediterranean (EURO MED)
Date of latest update: 2026-01-27

Description

Description (EN):
The StrategyMedFor project has gathered 7 PPs in 6 countries of the Interreg Cooperation area (including 2 IPA countries) plus relevant APs from the forest EU & international scene, to support mission 2 of the programme to protect, restore & valorise the Natural environment & Heritage. This project addresses particularly to the Mediterranean Forest Ecosystem. Attending EU & international strategies & policies about forests, we tackle needs & gaps of knowledge on strategies & action plans for the sustainable management of the Mediterranean forests. For that reason, the main goal of the StrategyMedFor is the development of a Strategy for Sustainable Management of Mediterranean Forests (SSMMF) targeted to each forest type & adapted to foreseeable climate scenarios. The novelty of the work is the integration of new technologies & wide-Mediterranean data into a spatial context, combining geospatial data, remote sensing, GIS & climate modelling in the development of the Strategy. The change we envisage is that our SSMMF will be a reference document for the next 10 years in the EU & Mediterranean scene, by providing scientific-sound recommendations about how & which forests to protect, restore or manage into resilience. Specific tools are planned, such as thematic cartography about the status of the forests in real time, & the priority areas for action, to make sustainable management more efficient at regional, national & local scale. We commit to the implementation of some of the results of the StrategyMedFor project by engaging operational partners in key test areas in countries (Eastern Mediterranean) where we identified scarce investment in research &/or protection of Mediterranean forests, an to transfer knowledge & consolidate cross-country collaborations to the broad Mediterranean region (N-Africa & MiddleEast). In addition, our results will be amplified to the Interreg community via the TCP, IDP & other communication channels of the programme.

Actual Achievements (EN):
During the first reporting period of the StrategyMedFor project, each partner contributed significantly to advancing the project's objectives across different work packages. ETC-UMA, as Lead Partner, played a key role in facilitating communication and coordination among all project partners. The LP made sure to agree with all partners and implement a major change in the Application form by exchanging one partner (DDIP ZDC) from Bosnia and Herzegovina with another (Faculty of Forestry - University of Sarajevo) as DDIP ZDC communicated their lack of capacities to remain a project partner. During this period, ETC-UMA organised general and bilateral meetings to kick off the project activities. A pre-kickoff meeting was held in February 2024 (online) and the kickoff meeting in March in Malaga. Both meetings were crucial for setting the governance structure and establishing a work and communication flow. ETC-UMA also led the technical aspects of WP2 by reviewing existing geospatial data and initiating a data archive, collaborating with partners like Istituto Oikos, CNPF, and CIHEAM-MAICh to establish criteria for cartographic content. Additionally, ETC-UMA supported communication activities by contributing to the Communication Strategy, developing project brochures, and creating drafts for story maps for the project and the pilot sites. Istituto Oikos participated in partnership meetings supporting activities in WP1, WP2, and WP4. At the kickoff meeting, they presented WP4 activities and organised the Annual Medforval Meeting in June 2024, introducing StrategyMedFor to the Medforval network. They also contributed to the understanding of High Ecological Value (HEV) forests for WP2 and launched a survey to prepare the structure of the MOOC under WP4. They prepared the groundwork for the dissemination of project knowledge through various online platforms. AIFM leads the external communication activities. They developed the project's Communication Strategy and Action Plan drafts and are validating them with project partners in addition to templates for communication material. They developed the project's brochure, and launched the official project website and social media channels. AIFM followed up on the Carbon Footprint Calculator tool, reporting issues and preparing for a seminar on its usage. Their participation in project meetings and events, such as the Natural Heritage Mission meeting in Rovinj, underscored their commitment to effective communication and outreach. CIHEAM-MAICh focused on initiating WP1 and supported some WP2 discussions. Under WP1, they are overseeing the shaping of associated criteria and indicators for a visionary Strategy for the Sustainable Management of Mediterranean Forests (SSMMF). They supported an online introductory workshop with associated partners that took place in June 2024 to identify the main criteria important for the MEditerranean forests that the Strategy needs to include. CIHEAM-MAICh also reviewed existing geospatial data for use in thematic mapping for the case study of Samaria Park that they lead on. PIGNP contributed to the creation of a data archive (D2.1.1), which will serve as a tool for evaluating the status of Mediterranean forests, identifying impacts, and facilitating forest protection, restoration, or management actions. For WP1, PIGNP provided assistance in communication activities (A1.5) by contributing content for the Storymap and content template related to Galicica NP. This contribution was essential for fulfilling deliverable D1.5.1, the "Communication Protocol on Exchanging Information and Processing the Participation of Stakeholders," fostering by this cross-border cooperation for the sustainable management of Mediterranean forest ecosystems. UNSA FoF leads WP3 effectively, coordinating activities related to the pilot sites as well as testing, validation, and implementation of project tools and methodologies. They completed necessary administrative tasks to join the project, established communication channels, and organised WP3 meetings focused on selecting pilot sites and planning future actions. UNSA FoF also participated in WP1 and WP2 activities, assisting in developing research questions and contributing to spatial data screening. Additionally, they supported communication efforts by creating up-to-date content for the project’s story maps and establishing social media profiles, further enhancing the project's visibility. CNPF were late in joining the partnership activities, as the main expert that was expected to join StrtegyMedFor was on long medical leave. Some replacements were ensured by CNPF for the most essential meetings in period 1. This situation is expected to improve in period 2 with the incorporation of the CNPF expert assigned to the project. The partnership has ensured a strong start to the StrategyMedFor project, with significant progress made in communication, technical preparation, and the initiation of key work packages. During Period 2, online and in-person meetings were essential for advancing project activities, sharing progress, and engaging stakeholders. A key event was the Consultation Workshop in Crete (September 17–19), where project advancements were presented, and stakeholder feedback was gathered. Partners also visited one project case study, the Samaria National Park. The visit was used as a demonstration exercise for the application of the IBP method in the field and to define priorities for restoration actions. Partners defined the work ahead to finalise communication strategy and action plan and confirmed the interest of using story maps and dashboards to communicate, through spatial data, project progressively. In November, the project team participated in the 8th Mediterranean Forest Week (Nov 4–7, 2024), and led a session focusing on StrategyMedFor C&I. The partnership presented two posters on the insights on Forest Europe Criteria and Indicators and an approach used and initial results of the spatialisation of Forest cover indicator. On November 8, the 2nd Steering Committee meeting gathered all partner organizations to plan upcoming activities. A working group on criteria and indicators was formed, including members from WP1, WP2, and the University of Copenhagen. Discussions with EFIMED explored synergies with the RessAlliance project. ETC-UMA, as LP, ensured smooth coordination, effective communication, and support for activities on criteria, indicators, and forest biodiversity. The SC meeting also helped resolving some administrative and budgetary questions that partners had, specifically PIGNP resolved the issue linked to the vehicle acquisition and vehicle issue and UNSA FoF clarified doubts about the budget linked to site level work. Under WP2, the deliverable “D.2.1.1 Data Archive” was developed and made available via the Mediterranean Biodiversity Protection Knowledge Platform (MBPKP), with ongoing updates. Progress on the local-scale cartographic material was discussed with partners, focusing on forest areas and fragmentation. Pilot site managers played a crucial role in validating the selected model. Some initial exchanges took place discussing the biodiversity parameters to use to assess forest biodiversity and ecological status. Istituto Oikos contributed to WP4, advancing the MOOC (A.4.1) and the Prespa-Ohrid Transboundary Management Plan (A.4.2). An anonymous questionnaire with 59 responses was launched, and collaboration with H2020 EU-Farmbook began. Discussions with key stakeholders took place for A.4.2, while feedback was provided on criteria and indicators. Oikos also validated maps and collected spatial data for WP2, reviewed the WP3 work plan, and participated in major project events. CNPF has participated in the Consultation Workshop with a presentation of the IBP, followed by a demonstration during the field visit. CNPF also started working on the literature review for the adaptation of the IBP to the Mediterranean context and actively participated in the Working Group meetings on Criteria and Indicators. As communication lead, AIFM worked on ensuring project visibility. The Communication Strategy and Action Plan were approved by partners in October 2024. Deliverables included multilingual posters and roll-up banners. A project presentation video is set for release on January 31, 2025. Social media engagement increased with five posts and two articles. AIFM joined the Chania workshop and organized collaboratively with partners the side event at the Mediterranean Forest Week in Barcelona. CIHEAM-MAICh reviewed strategies (A.1.1), defined forest management criteria and indicators, hosted and organized, collaboratively with partners, the first in-person consultation workshop in September 2024 (A.1.2). In A.1.3, progress was done within the WG on C&I to select criteria for geodata and started forest mapping for protection and resilience. They also ensured “green event” practices for carbon footprint monitoring (A.1.4) and contributed to communication strategy validation (A.1.5). PIGNP contributed to strategy development, criteria setting, and thematic information layers. PIGNP engaged in workshops, including a consultation in Greece and a Steering Committee meeting in Spain, presenting key insights on Galicica National Park. It collaborated with PrespaNet partners and informed stakeholders via the METT tool. Additionally, PIGNP submitted an annual report detailing 2024 activities to the Ministry of Environment of North Macedonia. UNSA FoF coordinated pilot site activities in Bosnia, Greece, & NM. They prepared a draft WP3 WP, helped develop criteria for sustainable forest management, and validated geospatial data. UNSA FoF maintained strong communication with partners and stakeholders, actively participating in meetings, workshops, and 8MFW. They also supported carbon footprint reporting, communication strategy, and promotional materials, improving project visibility. The StrategyMedFor project has made substantial progress during Period 3, consolidating its Mediterranean cooperation dimension and strengthening the technical, methodological, and communication foundations required to achieve its objectives. Under the coordination of the Lead Partner, several bilateral and joint meetings and one steering committee were organised during this reporting period to ensure consistency across work packages. These exchanges also facilitated the mobilisation of associated partners and target groups, who engaged actively through surveys, online sessions, and in-person workshops, thereby enriching the process with diverse perspectives and reinforcing its legitimacy. Major progress was made within WPs 1 and 2, and WP3 kicked off and developed its roadmap during this reporting period. Within WP1, a draft SSMMF and a list of Criteria and indicators were developed including a policy brief, technical report on criteria and indicators (C&I), and a user guide for forest managers. Consultation workshops, both online and in Sarajevo, gathered stakeholders to validate the framework and refine supplementary indicators. Contributions from partners such as UNSA FoF, Istituto Oikos, and PIGNP ensured that the C&I were tested against practical realities, with feedback drawn directly from pilot sites and park-level management experiences. Within WP2 a number of ancillary spatial data and indicators were tested, validated within the partnership, and produced at Mediterranean and local scales. Significant progress was achieved in the development of climate scenarios and the production of spatial data layers on forest area, fragmentation, landscape mosaics, and high ecological value. These outputs were discussed and validated closely to partners, in terms of methodology validation, result testing, etc. These results are used to inform the strategy’s technical framework and to provide a knowledge base for regional and local purposes. The testing sites, with a special effort made by PIGNP, supported this effort by collecting additional data and by validating local geospatial datasets and preparing species lists (specially for Galicica National Park), adding site-specific expertise to the regional database. WP3 kicked off in this reporting period, and several partners meetings were made to set up a roadmap for action and site level activities started in the 3 sites. A major milestone was the Index of Biodiversity Potential (IBP) training organised by the CNPF in Toulouse in April. The training on the use of IBP for partners and associated partners was designed to train the group on the IBP applicability. After the training, several partners applied the IBP method in several pre-identified plots with the guidance of the CNPF. During this period, field activities focusing on adapting the IBP definition to the Balkan region and testing it in Bosnia and Herzegovina were made through a collaborative effort between UNSA FoF and CNPF. Action plans for pilot sites in Bosnia and Herzegovina, Greece, and North Macedonia were prepared. The second regional consultation workshop took place in Sarajevo during which a field visit to the Dinaric Alps enabled direct validation of indicators and methodologies to develop spatial indicators. PIGNP began implementing its pilot site activities, preparing timelines for Grecian juniper conservation measures, and engaging in a transboundary planning process with partners from Albania and Greece. This collaboration is expected to result in a joint conservation plan for the Prespa/Ohrid region, demonstrating the project’s capacity to foster cross-border solutions for shared ecological challenges. Istituto Oikos advanced Work Package 4 by coordinating the design of the project’s Massive Open Online Course (MOOC) on Mediterranean forest protection, restoration, and sustainable management. Structured in multiple interactive modules, the MOOC is being developed in close alignment with the Interreg Euro-MED Academy platform. Oikos started the preparations for the upcoming Prespa/Ohrid transboundary workshop, and contributed by supporting the spatialisation attempts of the High Ecological Value index. On the communication front, AIFM ensured project visibility and stakeholder engagement through the production of promotional videos, news articles, and social media content, alongside the design of consultation documents and event facilitation tools. Its contributions included the production of a highlight video during the Sarajevo workshop and the development of a publication calendar to strategically disseminate project outputs. Spending deviations this period were minor. AIFM and CIHEAM-MAICh had small changes due to activity timing and resource adjustments and PIGNP could do the purchase of the vehicle (this cost is included in PR 3) and balance the previous deviation and underspending. Overall, these are timing/efficiency issues and don’t affect project implementation.
