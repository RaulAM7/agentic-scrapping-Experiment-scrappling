Clepsydra - Groundwater monitoring and Decision Support System development to optimise decision-making in sensitive and water-scarce agricultural environments in the Mediterranean context

Programme 2021 - 2027 Interreg VI-B EURO Mediterranean (EURO MED)
Date of latest update: 2026-01-27

Description

Description (FR):
Une clepsydre est une horloge à eau, où l'eau mesure le temps et, dans ce projet c'est l'eau qui sera "mesurée" dans le temps. En effet, il existe un besoin en amélioration des réseaux de surveillance des aquifères conventionnels, contrôlant principalement la zone saturée de l'aquifère et ayant une valeur limitée pour évaluer l'impact des mesures de prévention de la pollution. Ensuite, par le manque de convivialité des modèles numériques d'écoulement utilisés comme outils de prévision. Il est donc tout aussi crucial de disposer d'un outil intelligent pour interpréter les données hydrogéologiques que de sensibiliser la société civile à l'importance des eaux souterraines. Clepsydra vise à développer et tester un système intelligent de surveillance et d'interprétation des données hydrogéologiques sur le comportement des aquifères, y compris développer un système d'aide à la décision - DSS, avec une attention particulière à la qualité des eaux souterraines en agriculture. En améliorant les réseaux de surveillance actuels et en développant un DSS, les professionnels de l'eau et de l'agriculture pourront prendre des décisions respectueuses de l'environnement sur la base de données scientifiques fiables. Dans le projet, la coopération et l’implication des parties prenantes de la quadruple hélice sont un principe directeur pour générer des résultats à fort impact. Le projet implique 9 partenaires de 6 pays Med (PT, ES, FR, IT, MT & EL), représentant la sphère publique, académique, agricole, des utilisateurs d’eaux souterraines et un cluster de l'eau. Dans les sites pilotes, le projet Clepsydra exploitera le potentiel d'innovation du DSS et l'alignera sur les pratiques améliorées de surveillance des eaux souterraines. Les parties prenantes de la 4helix seront impliquées dans le projet et des partenariats public-privé seront générés avec l'implication des utilisateurs finaux et du secteur privé dans l'amélioration des réseaux de surveillance.

Description (EN):
A clepsydra is a water clock, where water measures the time and, in this project it is the water which will be "measured" in time. In fact, there is a need to improve conventional aquifers monitoring networks which mainly control aquifer saturated zone, having a limited value for the assessment of the impact of pollution prevention measures taken. Additionally, a smart tool to interpret hydrogeological data is needed because current numerical flow models to predict groundwater pollution and level is not friendly for all users. Also, raising awareness of civil society about the importance of aquifers is needed. Clepsydra aims to develop and test a smart system for monitoring and interpreting hydrogeological data on the behaviour of aquifers (including the development of a Decision Support System - DSS), with special attention to groundwater quality in agriculture to be able to make decisions. By improving current monitoring networks and developing a DSS, water and agriculture professionals will be able to make environmentally friendly decisions based on reliable scientific data. In the project, the increased cooperation and capacities of the 4helix stakeholders is a guiding principle to generate project results with greater impact. The project involves 9 partners from 6 Med countries (PT, ES, FR, IT, MT & EL), made up of public authorities, academia, agriculture, groundwater users and a water cluster. In the pilot sites, Clepsydra Project will harness the innovation potential of the DSS and align it with upgraded groundwater monitoring practices. 4helix stakeholders will be involved in the project and public-private partnerships will be generated with the incorporation of final users and private sector in the improvement of monitoring networks.

Actual Achievements (EN):
During the first reporting period, our team made significant progress across several key activities, contributing to the overall objectives of Clepsydra Project. We focused on establishing strong coordination and communication channels among all partners to ensure smooth collaboration. This included participating in regular virtual meetings, setting up communication and document sharing protocols and ensuring all partners were aligned with the project’s objectives and timelines. The implementation of the work programme was focused on WP1 on assessing of monitoring capacities in pilot areas and identification of key gaps. In fact, partners where pilot will be implemented, have started to select the members of their local living labs. We also engaged with stakeholders through virtual communications and meetings, gathering valuable feedback that helped shape the project’s approach and outputs. On the other hand, partners agreed on the approach and template to be filled in about the comparative analysis of the control and monitoring of existing networks in pilot sites whose results will be the base for the design of the following project activities. In parallel, partners also made the first steps to calculate the Water and carbon footprint of the control. In addition to that, Clepsydra has started to design the Groundwater Open Days which will be finally implemented in the following semester. Overall, our progress during this period has laid a solid foundation for the continued success of Clepsydra Project. During the first year, the Clepsydra project has made steady and substantial progress, moving from initial coordination activities to the first technical and preparatory steps toward achieving its core objectives. The project aims to develop an innovative, AI-based Decision Support System (DSS) and improve groundwater monitoring networks to enhance decision-making in water-scarce agricultural environments across the Mediterranean. The progress achieved so far reflects the strength of transnational cooperation, with each partner contributing expertise, data, and insights to ensure shared goals are met. The first phase of the project focused on building a solid foundation for collaboration. The partnership established strong internal communication structures, including monthly virtual coordination meetings, efficient document-sharing platforms, and clear timelines to keep all partners aligned. In Work Package 1 (WP1), the focus was on assessing the monitoring capacities in pilot areas and identifying key gaps. With the coordination of Aqua Valley, partners leading pilot sites set up their Local Living Labs (LLLs), bringing together stakeholders from public authorities, the private sector, research institutions, and civil society. The first meetings of these LLLs have been crucial in gathering feedback and ensuring that project actions respond to real local needs. Partners also collaborated to prepare a comparative analysis of existing monitoring networks in each pilot area led by IGME-CSIC, a task that will feed directly into the design of future monitoring protocols and DSS functionality. Parallel to this, partners began calculating the water and carbon footprints associated with current monitoring practices, supporting the project’s commitment to sustainability. Another notable achievement in WP1 was the launch of the Groundwater Open Days, with most partners successfully organizing these public events to raise awareness of groundwater issues among local communities. In Work Package 2 (WP2), key progress has been made in designing the Decision Support System (DSS), which is the project’s main technical output. All partners contributed by providing critical data from their pilot sites and defining the main caracheristics of the tool, enabling UNICAS to start developing the predictive models. UNICAS has already made significant headway by testing several AI-based models, focusing particularly on forecasting groundwater levels (GWL), identified as the most relevant parameter for the DSS. This collaborative approach highlights the added value of transnational cooperation, as data collected from diverse Mediterranean environments enriches the accuracy and robustness of the models. In Work Package 3 (WP3), after conducting the detailed assessments of existing monitoring networks, partners identified key gaps and began planning improvements. Preparations are underway for acquiring and installing new monitoring instruments, tailored to the specific needs of each pilot site. Additionally, partners continued soil monitoring in agricultural areas, maintaining consistent data collection that will feed into the DSS. Work Package 4 (WP4) also saw early but important progress, led by IRD. The focus has been on laying the groundwork for capacity building and raising social awareness. Activities have included the selection of study sites, mapping stakeholder profiles, and starting the development of educational materials and video content aimed at explaining groundwater management challenges and solutions in an accessible way. IRD’s work ensures that the project’s technical results are not confined to specialists but are translated into messages that can reach broader audiences and help drive behavior change. Communication activities have advanced significantly, with key tools developed. Aqua Valley has finalised the project’s communication strategy, created poster and roll-up templates, launched the project website and social media channels, and begun work on the first project video. This communication effort ensures that both project stakeholders and the general public are kept informed and engaged. A highlight of this period was the project’s presence at an influential international event: IGME-CSIC and EWA’s participation in the Water Framework Directive: 46th CIS-Groundwater Working Group Meeting in Budapest (November 2024). Presenting Clepsydra at this high-level forum allowed the project to gain visibility among policymakers and experts, demonstrating the relevance of the project’s objectives and approach within the broader European water governance context. During this reporting period, one key deviation from the original work plan concerned the delay in finalising Deliverable D2.1.1 – Groundwater Monitoring Protocol for the Pilot Sites. After starting the drafting process, it became evident that the development of this protocol is significantly more complex than initially anticipated. The protocol must be applicable across four pilot sites with very different hydrogeological, climatic, and operational characteristics. As a result, it is essential to carefully adapt technical content to ensure the recommendations are relevant, feasible, and scientifically sound in each local context. Moreover, the protocol must go beyond general guidelines. It is expected to harmonise diverse technical methodologies, sampling procedures, and data quality criteria that meet both regulatory requirements and research standards. Given this level of complexity, the responsible team, led by IGME-CSIC, made the decision to prioritise the technical robustness and coherence of the document over speed of delivery. Work on the protocol continues actively, and a final version is expected in the coming months. The partnership appreciates the Joint Secretariat’s understanding, as this delay reflects the ambition to produce a high-quality and impactful result. On the financial side, some delays in spending have also been observed compared to the original forecast. These deviations are mainly due to the longer-than-expected duration and complexity of public procurement procedures for equipment acquisition. As a result, certain planned expenses have been postponed. Nevertheless, all procurement processes are now underway or nearing completion. IGME-CSIC, as project lead partner, is closely monitoring the financial execution of each partner and their updated forecasts for the upcoming semesters. This will help ensure alignment with the overall budget and allow corrective measures to be taken early if needed to safeguard project performance.

    Contribution to wider strategies and policies:

    • EuropeanGreenDeal Clepsydra will contribute to the European Green Deal objectives of clean water and healthy and affordable food to improve citizens' well-being.
    • As a subsurface reservoir, an aquifer enhances the resilience of the water cycle, reduces treatment requirements for drinking water, and mitigates environmental hazards.
    • For the period 2016-2019, across Member States, 14.1% of groundwater still exceeded the nitrates concentration limit set for drinking water.
    • The monitoring frameworks and DSS created in the project will provide an early warning system, allowing for more tailored and immediate decisions that will ultimately avoid contamination and help maintain the groundwater in better conditions.
    • Additionally, Clepsydra will contribute to the following strategies and plans:
    • - Farm-to-fork strategy (EC2020a), by helping agronomical activities reduce their impact on the groundwater and groundwater-dependent ecosystems (GDE) and biodiversity and the environment.
    • - EU biodiversity strategy (EC2020b), since the project will contribute to regulating groundwater abstraction helping preserve water flows that affect GDE, thus avoiding their disruption through human activity.
    • - Zero pollution Action Plan (EC2021b) These are actually shared objectives with Clepsydra as it aims to monitor the quality of groundwater (including nutrients) to allow for more tailored decisions in agricultural activities through one of the main objectives of this plan: the reduction of nutrient losses and the improvement of water.
    • - Climate change adaptation strategy (EC 2021a) because the importance of groundwater is emphasised in the context of climate change with recurrent and persistent droughts. Clepsydra aims to avoid the effects of human activity in groundwater improving the monitoring and the results obtained from the DSS may help us understand the relationship between groundwater and climate, as well as preserve ecological flows.
    • Other The Nitrates Directive (91/676/EEC)
    • Various European Commission documents indicate that little progress has been made by the Member States in the fight against nitrate pollution, especially as far as aquifers are concerned. For this project, the principle of necessity can be invoked, which has its raison d'être in the obligation to provide a more forceful response to the problem of diffuse pollution, which is necessary in view of the poor results obtained so far in the fight against this problem.
    • Clepsydra contributes to the implementation of the nitrates Directive in groundwater bodies through monitoring and the development of a DSS that will provide an early warning system. The monitoring protocol, networks, and DSS aim to help understand the path of nitrates from the soil to the aquifer and to take the necessary actions to prevent leaching based on real-time scientific data.
    • The implementation of the project will contribute to better identify polluted or at risk of pollution groundwater bodies, establishing the appropriate measures for agriculture and other practices based on the current situation with the help of the DSS, and improving monitoring and reporting on nitrates including estimation of future trends.
    • Other Directive 2006/118/EC on the protection of groundwater against pollution and deterioration
    • The Groundwater Directive objectives are at the core of Clepsydra as it precisely aims to protect groundwater in the Mediterranean area from potential pollution from agriculture and other practices by accurate monitoring, improvement of the monitoring network, and interpretation of data through a DSS.
    • The inclusion of the unsaturated zone is an additional monitoring parameter that is included in this project, this area has been barely studied and it is crucial to understand the paths pollutants follow from the soil to the groundwater.
    • Clepsydra will provide a complementary monitoring tool to support the adoption of the GWD’s Prevent and Limit Objectives, particularly by providing insights on the beneficial impact of the unsaturated zone.
    • Other EU Water Framework Directive (2000/60/EC)
    • Clepsydra contributes to the implementation of WFD requirements to monitor groundwater bodies so as to detect changes in chemical composition, and to reverse any induced upward pollution trend from human activities as well as impacts of exploitation and recharge. It will therefore contribute to the River Basin Management Plans implementation.
    • Clepsydra will support the WFD in providing information to transparently determine extensions to the good status objectives based on natural conditions.
    • Of particular relevance, also, Clepsydra will enable the estimation of nitrate loading from today’s land use activities, hence enabling the projection of the path to good qualitative status.
    • Quantity is also a major issue for groundwater. There is only a certain amount of recharge into groundwater each year, and of this recharge, some is needed to support connected ecosystems (whether they are surface water bodies or terrestrial systems such as wetlands). For good management, only that portion of the overall recharge not needed by the ecology can be abstracted, this is the sustainable resource, and the Directive limits abstraction to that quantity. Continuous monitoring and the DSS will support management bodies so their decisions match the requirements and protect the aquifer.
    • In particular it affects to the following articles:
    • Article 4 in terms of improving the reliability of status assessments.
    • Article 8 Monitoring of surface water status, groundwater status and protected areas: Clepsydra will provide a complimentary monitoring framework to that required by the Directive – increasing confidence in status assessment
    • Article 11 Programme of measures: Clepsydra will increase capacity to assess the effectivity of measures.
    • MediterraneanSeaBasin
    • For the implementation of Clepsydra, a close cooperation between international partners is vital to develop common tools and share best practices, this is one of the goals of this strategy. The new DSS will support professionals in taking adequate decisions to protect and improve aquifers situation what will contribute to the blue economy. At the same time, the preservation of ecosystems and biodiversity (one of the purposes of this Initiative) is achieved by controlling the flows of contaminants in GDE such as rivers, wetlands, etc, which can be accomplished by the outputs of the proposed project.
    • Other Common Agricultural Policy
    • The CAP aims to promote synergies between a healthy environment and agriculture. In case of groundwater, it is a valuable resource for agriculture, industry, tourism and other sectors, however, the aquifers have been damaged through their exploitation and pollution. Clepsydra aims to develop innovative tools to reverse these situations through more efficient management that benefits both parties. One example is the use of nitrates, if farmers are able to measure the amount of nitrates leaching below the roots, they will be able to better estimate the fertilisers needed so leaching to the aquifer doesn’t happen and they avoid added costs. This is a mutually beneficial initiative, and cooperation with farmers should be sought.
    • Other EU Missions
    • The proposed Project is at the heart of EU Missions, in particular, in the Adaptation to Climate Change Mission, because the Clepsydra will entail more sustainable agricultural activities; the Restore our Ocean and Waters Mission to contribute to fighting against water pollution through monitoring and a DSS to support decision making, and the Soil Deal for Europe, dealing with soil protection and soil restoration.
    • Other Agenda 2030 for sustainable development
    • Agenda 2030 focuses largely on the protection of the environment, and Clepsydra aims to contribute to this through the protection of groundwater. To be more specific, this project will mainly contribute to the Sustainable Development Goal 6 Clean water and sanitation. Clepsydra will provide innovative tools to public authorities and water management organisations that will monitor quality and quantity values in groundwater and interpret the obtained data to facilitate decision-making (6.5) resulting in a better quality of groundwater (6.1) with the reduction of leached pollutants (6.3) and more efficient use of groundwater resources minimising the effects of exploitation and recharge. Through the project, we expect to help improve the groundwater bodies and this will inevitably impact the Groundwater Dependent Ecosystems (connected lagunes, rivers, sea, etc) and the biodiversity of these areas (6.6).
    • Clepsydra involves stakeholders from the 4helix among its partners and associated organisations, with the aim to involve them in the solutions developed. (6.b 6.a) One of the main objectives of the project is to raise awareness of the importance of aquifers, the problems they face and the solutions developed among all stakeholders including general society through socio-educational outreach and training. (6.a)

    • TerritorialAgenda2030 The Territorial Agenda 2030 addresses the needs created by climate change and sustainable development, among the areas to which it aims to respond and on which Clepsydra will have an important effect are the quality of air, soil and water and indirectly the loss of biodiversity and soil consumption.
