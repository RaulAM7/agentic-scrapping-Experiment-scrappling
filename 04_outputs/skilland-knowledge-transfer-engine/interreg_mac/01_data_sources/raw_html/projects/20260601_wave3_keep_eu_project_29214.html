Wetlands-based solutions for climate change adaptation, risk prevention and mitigation

Programme 2021 - 2027 Interreg VI-B EURO Mediterranean (EURO MED)
Date of latest update: 2026-01-27

Description

Description (EN):
Adaptation to the consequences of climate change is a key challenge in the EuroMed territory. Several opportunities arise to reinforce adaptation by shifting our attention to NbS and among them “wetland-based solutions”. Wetland loss and degradation is a sign that the recognition of the wetland climate services as a catalyst for the improvement of the quality of human life and sustainable growth, is probably weak at the various governance levels.Wetland4Change aims to validate transferable solutions based on wetland conservation and restoration for climate adaptation and mitigation, in order to accelerate the capacities of wetland managers and of policy makers to cope with the climate crisis at local and transnational level, by improving take up of science-based knowledge and governance mechanisms, through assessments, guidance, capacity building and experience exchange. The main project outputs are: two wetland-based solutions tested and validated in 5 pilot sites at 5 countries (BG, GR, IT, FR, ES): for carbon-sequestration and for flood regulation. On top of this a dynamic user-oriented transfer plan, powered by science-based knowledge, will be developed for the first time; directly linked to the long term potential for cooperating across borders. The project goes beyond existing practices through creation of the EuroMED Wetland knowledge base (new knowledge on wetland location, extent, condition, and ecosystem services); the novelty in assessing carbon sequestration and flood regulation capacities of wetlands for national authority obligations (i.e GAEC 2 for the agriculture sector), the experimental transnational hub for wetland climate solutions. As a result, the project will improve the knowledge and the management capacities of the involved stakeholders to promote and plan wetlands protection and conservation as nature-based solutions in climate change adaptation, mitigation and risk prevention.

Description (FR):
Faire face au changement climatique est un défi majeur en Méditerranée et les Solutions fondées sur la Nature (SfN) représentent de réelles opportunités pour une meilleure adaptation et atténuation de ses effets, en particulier celles fondées sur les zones humides. Cependant, ces écosystèmes déclinent très rapidement dans cette région et leur sauvegarde, à travers des actions de conservation et de restauration, est une urgence vitale. Le projet Wetland4Change vise donc à valider des solutions transférables, axées sur la conservation et la restauration des zones humides pour l'adaptation au changement climatique et l'atténuation de ses effets, afin d’aider les gestionnaires et les décideurs à mieux faire face à cette crise, notamment en améliorant l’intégration des connaissances scientifiques dans les mécanismes de gestion et de gouvernance, par le biais d'évaluations, de conseils, de développement des capacités et d'échanges d'expériences. Les principaux résultats du projet seront : deux solutions basées sur les zones humides, testées et validées dans 5 sites pilotes de 5 pays (BG, GR, IT, FR, ES), pour la séquestration du carbone et la régulation des crues. En outre, un plan de transfert orienté vers les utilisateurs, alimenté par des connaissances scientifiques et intégrant la coopération transfrontalière, sera également développé. Le projet ira au-delà des pratiques existantes, avec la création d’une base de connaissances EuroMED sur les écosystèmes humides (localisation, étendue, état et services écosystémiques) et intégrera des innovations telles que l’estimation des services écosystémiques de séquestration du carbone et de régulation des crues de ces habitats. En conséquence, le projet améliorera les connaissances et les capacités des parties prenantes impliquées, afin de promouvoir et planifier la conservation et la restauration des zones humides en tant que SfN pour l'adaptation au changement climatique, l'atténuation et la prévention des risques.

Actual Achievements (EN):
The Wetland4Change project has made significant strides in its early stages, with a strong focus on developing methodologies for testing and validating wetland-based solutions - carbon sequestration and flood regulation, as well as fostering collaboration between project partners and stakeholders. The project team was fully established, and a structured workflow was created to ensure smooth coordination both internally and among project partners. A detailed schedule for meetings, events, and deliverables was finalized, and the Project Steering Committee was formed to oversee governance. The Internal Management Handbook was developed and shared with the partners. The project officially launched with a kick-off event in Sofia, Bulgaria, in April 2024. The event included a visit to Dragoman Marsh, Bulgaria's largest karst wetland, emphasizing the project’s focus on wetland ecosystems. Collaboration extended across borders, with representatives actively participating in training and knowledge exchange sessions, particularly through the Summer School on ecosystem restoration. Partners presented on wetlands' importance, restoration techniques, and how to create effective restoration plans, further building capacity among participants. Project representative participated also in the joint thematic projects meeting in Croatia. The work in the pilot sites advanced with gathering and harmonizing essential data for the two main deliverables for the reporting period, preliminary data and technical information were collected. The data collection followed the specific recommendations provided by leaders of Work Package 1 (Carbon Sequestration) and Work Package 2 (Flood Regulation), ensuring that the information met project needs for the initial stages of the assessments. Two deliverables are delivered: Del. 1.1.1: Preliminary study and technical guidance report for the mapping and assessment of C-sequestration and Del. 2.1.1. Report for the mapping and assessment of the flood regulation wetland service. The project's communication activities were successfully launched, including the creation of a Communication Strategy and various tools such as roll-ups, leaflets, and an online presence through social media and a dedicated webpage. Public engagement included participation in local events, such as the Kerkini Lake Run in Greece, helping to raise awareness of the project’s goals and engage local communities. The project has fostered strong cooperation between partners from various regions, enhancing knowledge exchange and collective problem-solving. The main outputs delivered are: i) Preliminary reports and methodologies for carbon sequestration and flood regulation; ii) Communication tools and platforms to engage stakeholders and the public; iii) Training and educational materials developed for ecosystem restoration and wetland management. The Wetland4Change project benefits from the collaboration of diverse partners across Europe, who bring unique perspectives and expertise to the table. By working together, the project is able to tackle complex environmental challenges more effectively, drawing on shared knowledge and resources to develop innovative wetland-based solutions. This cooperation not only enriches the project’s outputs but also ensures that the solutions developed have wide applicability and impact across the region. During the last two reporting periods, significant progress was achieved across key work packages (WPs), advancing wetland-based solutions for carbon sequestration and flood regulation. Through extensive research, stakeholder engagement, and technical training, the project has made substantial contributions to the implementation and validation of Nature-based Solutions (NbS) in pilot areas. WP1: Wetland-Based Solution for Carbon Sequestration We conducted preliminary research, gathered critical data, and refined the methodological framework for assessing carbon sequestration potential. Key highlights include: Participation in the Technical Training Workshop in Valencia, where partners gained insights into the Albufera Carbon Sequestration Methodology, cause-and-effect matrix prioritization, and survey plans for monitoring carbon sequestration capacity. Development of the Preliminary Study and Technical Guidance Report for Carbon Sequestration Mapping, ensuring methodological consistency across pilot sites. Field data collection and sampling strategy development, along with stakeholder networking for knowledge sharing and support. Scientific review and assessment of wetland carbon sequestration dynamics, strengthening expertise within the project team. WP2: Wetland-Based Solution for Flood Regulation Key achievements include: Networking with local and regional water management authorities to assess existing flood risk management frameworks and explore the integration of NbS. Collection and organization of hydrological, ecological, and governance-related data necessary for WP2 deliverables. Development of a structured methodology to assess the role of wetlands in flood regulation, prepared during this reporting period to support future project activities. Preparation for the internal technical workshop and online training organized by the University of Malaga and Tour du Valat, ensuring a solid methodological foundation for subsequent research and implementation. Stakeholder Engagement and Policy Advocacy Collaboration with stakeholders was a priority, emphasizing the added value of NbS for climate resilience. Notable activities included: Hosting the first stakeholder meetings in the pilot sites, engaging municipalities, NGOs, and academia to discuss wetland conservation strategies. During the events, discussions centred on wetland services, climate adaptation, and synergies with agricultural policies and tourism. Establishing a dialogue with key regional and national authorities to integrate NbS into flood risk management plans and climate adaptation strategies. WP3: The transfer plan of wetland-based climate change adaptation and mitigation solutions Knowledge exchange and replication efforts were supported through: Development of the Transfer Case template in collaboration, ensuring a structured approach for sharing best practices. Active participation in policy advocacy forums, including the Interreg Euro-MED Working Groups and the Natural Heritage Mission Institutional Dialogue Event in Malaga. Contributions to the joint position paper on restoration, leveraging momentum from the recently approved EU Nature Restoration Regulation. Ongoing engagement with associated partners and local stakeholders to promote wetland restoration solutions for agricultural and climate policies. Preparation of training material for and participation to the Natural Heritage Mission’s 1st Summer School on the topic ““Our Mediterranean ecosystems’ health is in danger. Time to restore it!” Project Management and Communication The project maintained effective coordination and dissemination efforts by: Submitting the first progress report, ensuring continuous monitoring and transparent reporting. Facilitating seamless information exchange with project partners, the Joint Secretariat, and the Managing Authority. Organizing brainstorming sessions on key messages, reinforcing a coherent and impactful communication strategy. Publishing updates on project achievements, pilot site activities, and stakeholder engagements. The reporting period has been marked by substantial progress in implementing and validating wetland-based solutions for carbon sequestration and flood regulation. Through extensive stakeholder collaboration, technical advancements, and policy advocacy, the project is paving the way for sustainable climate adaptation strategies in Mediterranean wetland ecosystems. These efforts underscore the vital role of wetlands in mitigating climate change impacts and highlight the collective commitment to integrating NbS into regional and EU-wide policies. During the first half of 2025, Wetland4Change advanced the testing of wetland-based solutions for climate adaptation across five pilot territories in Bulgaria, Greece, Italy, Spain and France, building a shared evidence base and creating momentum among end-users. The project continued to pursue two complementary solutions—carbon sequestration and flood regulation—through harmonised methods and joint validation, demonstrating why a transnational approach is essential in the Euro-MED context. For the carbon-sequestration solution, partners progressed along two tracks. First, harmonised protocols and data workflows coordinated with the WP1 leader enabled partners to assemble site data and prepare modelling inputs for all pilots. Second, targeted field campaigns were launched where feasible to strengthen calibration. In Greece, EKBY designed and carried out the first carbon-flux sampling in Lake Kerkini, building a floating chamber with a CO₂ sensor and collecting measurements at 20 locations alongside supporting environmental parameters to inform Tier-2 modelling of the whole wetland. n Italy, measurements were taken in the Oristano pilot during a visit coordinated with UVEG, resolving early data gaps and improving the accuracy of ongoing GHG calculations. In Bulgaria, the team focused on monitoring and satellite analyses and scheduled the first GHG field measurements for September in coordination with UVEG, ensuring methodological alignment ahead of field deployment. In France and Spain, partners concentrated on data preparation and coordination rather than on-site flux campaigns in this semester. Together, these steps enriched the common inputs that will feed the forthcoming technical report, maps and communication materials on wetland carbon capacity. The flood-regulation solution reached a major milestone with the finalisation and roll-out of a common methodology led by Tour du Valat, which compiled and, where necessary, produced missing datasets for all pilots and shared an implementation protocol with the partnership. A technical workshop hosted by UMA in Málaga in January consolidated key elements—such as integrating soil data and refining index weighting—and an online follow-up in March aligned partners for implementation. First mapping and assessment results were then discussed jointly for Oristano, Albufera and Kerkini at the General Assembly, and presented to stakeholders during the first Living Lab. A highlight of the period was the Living Lab in Oristano, organised by MEDSEA, which gathered local authorities, administrations, researchers, private actors and community representatives. The event combined co-design discussions with in-field demonstrations, showcased the progress on both solutions, and strengthened a shared understanding of how results can be integrated into local planning and management. This hands-on format accelerated learning across sites and ensured that technical choices reflect user needs. Policy and governance engagement deepened in parallel. In Bulgaria, the Ministry of Environment and Water and the Municipality of Blagoevgrad engaged actively with the pilot, including interest in adding a new wetland and initiating its protection, and participation in a national working group on GAEC 2 to link wetland carbon storage with the CAP framework. In Greece, EKBY mobilised regional and municipal authorities and involved a national policy maker through the Network of Greek Cities with Lakes, orienting results toward flood-risk and water-management plans. Across the partnership, links with the Natural Heritage Mission’s Amplification Room were strengthened to ensure that tested outputs are transferred and up-scaled, with UMA coordinating result-amplification actions and preparing contributions for training and clustering activities. Overall, by mid-2025 the project has delivered a shared methodology for flood regulation, put in place harmonised workflows for carbon assessment, launched field validation where conditions allowed, and mobilised stakeholders through a living lab that connected science and practice. The cooperation adds value by providing comparable methods across diverse wetlands, pooling modelling and Earth-observation expertise with on-the-ground management, and threading results into policy arenas from local municipalities to national CAP implementation. This collective effort keeps Wetland4Change on track to deliver robust carbon and flood-regulation assessments, a Euro-MED wetland knowledge base, and a dynamic, stakeholder-oriented Transfer Plan that can be taken up beyond the pilot sites

    Contribution to wider strategies and policies:

    • EUStrategyAdriaticIonianRegion Climate change is expected to affect much of the Adriatic- Ionian Region more severely than elsewhere in the EU, especially in the case of coastal natural habitats. As denoted in the Topic 2 of this strategy (Transnational terrestrial habitats and biodiversity), its action plan focuses on well-identified EU priorities such as mitigation and adaptation to climate change, issues related to water quality and disaster risk reduction. All these aspects are jointly addressed in the present project. On the other hand, two partners of the project (EKBY and MEDSEA) belong to countries within this strategy (Greece and Italy, respectively), which will contribute to reinforce the development of macro-regional actions as it is also encouraged by this strategy.
    • EuropeanGreenDeal The project will support the EU Green Deal by contributing to two of its main objectives: 1) Increasing the EU’s climate ambition for 2030 and 2050 and 2) Preserving and restoring ecosystems and biodiversity. The latter will be supported by the tools and strategies developed at pilot area level which will ensure wetland conservation and restoration, with a direct positive impact on the mitigation capacity of wetlands supporting the EU’s climate ambition. The EU Biodiversity Strategy for 2030 is a key element of the EU Green Deal. The Strategy refers explicitly to wetland protection and restoration as essential nature-based solutions for emission reduction and climate adaptation, taking into account projected shifts in vegetation zones, and highlights the protection of coastal and floodplain wetlands for reducing flood damage losses. ETC-UMA is part of the EC Expert Group on (Ecosystem) monitoring - EC EGM- that will guide EC and EU MSs throughout the implementation phase of the EC Biodiversity Strategy to 2030. ETC-UMA will continue cross-feeding the Mediterranean development in guiding ecosystem management in the Mediterranean with the developments of the EC EGM for wetland. This will ensure harmonisation and uptake between the EU and the Mediterranean processes and will guide restoration prioritisation and best practices.
    • TerritorialAgenda2030 The Territorial Agenda provides an action-oriented framework to promote territorial cohesion in Europe. Accordingly, the present proposal is expected to foster the convergence and equality in the EU policies and programmes dedicated to environmental monitoring, furthermore, introducing the climatic component to this. Particularly, this strategy encourages developing actions to offset climate change effects, considering that mitigation and adaptation actions can also bring new development opportunities for places.
