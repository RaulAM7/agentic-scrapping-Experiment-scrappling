COOL NOONS

Programme 2021 - 2027 Interreg VI-B EURO Mediterranean (EURO MED)
Date of latest update: 2026-01-27

Description

Description (EN):
Summer 2022: Lisbon 43°, Athens 45°, Syracuse 48°.... All the experts agree. Summer 2022 has given us a foretaste of what the future climate will be like in the Mediterranean, a climate in which extremes will become more pronounced and heat waves will continue to multiply. Under these conditions, who will dare to visit Mediterranean cities during the months of July and August? Who will still be walking around Marseille or Bologna in the middle of summer? The Cool Noons project aims to address the issue of urban tourism in Mediterranean cities, dramatically exposed to the negative effects of climate change and in particular to heat waves. . How to renew the urban tourism offer by adapting it to a context of extreme and rapid changes? How to improve the comfort of cities in summer for the benefit of tourists and inhabitants? How can we ensure the sustainability of an economic activity as fundamental as tourism in Mediterranean countries? In Cool Noons project, five pilot Mediterranean cities will test solutions to improve the offer of activities for both tourists and inhabitants during the hottest hours of the day. Some of the solutions to be tested have already demonstrated their effectiveness (in contexts not linked with tourism) such as revegetation, water equipment or light-colored urban surfaces. Other new solutions will emerge from the creativity of the end "users" of the cities, i.e. the residents and tourists. The solutions tested will also be cross evaluated to clarify their potential for replication in the Mediterranean region. Cool Noons is an innovative project for the way it addresses an urgent and underestimated issue such as the evolution of urban tourism towards a more sustainable model. It is also innovative for the tools and methods used, which combine scientific accuracy, pragmatism and creativity, thanks to the diversity of the partners involved.

Description (FR):
Eté 2022 : Lisbonne 43°, Athènes 45°, Syracuse 48°…. Tous les experts s’accordent à dire que l’été 2022 nous a offert un avant-goût de ce que le climat futur sera en Méditerranée, un climat dans lequel les extrêmes s’accentueront et les vagues de chaleurs ne cesseront de se multiplier. Dans ces conditions qui osera visiter les villes pendant les mois de juillet et août ? Qui se baladera encore en plein été à Marseille ou Bologne ? Cool Noons se propose de traiter la question du tourisme urbain dans les villes méditerranéennes fortement exposées aux effets néfastes du changement climatique et notamment aux vagues de chaleurs. Comment renouveler l’offre de tourisme urbain en l’adaptant à un contexte de changements aussi extrêmes que rapides ? Comment améliorer le confort des villes l’été au bénéfice des touristes et des habitants ? Comment assurer la pérennité d’une activité économique aussi structurante que le tourisme en Méditerranée? Dans Cool Noons, cinq villes pilotes méditerranéennes de différents pays testeront des solutions pour améliore l’accueil des visiteurs pendant les heures plus chaudes de la journée. Des solutions déjà connues et reconnues comme efficaces telles que la végétalisation, l’aménagement des points d’eau ou les revêtements urbains de couleurs claires mais aussi des solutions nouvelles qui émergeront de la créativité des « usagers » des villes, à savoir les résidents et les touristes. Les solutions testées feront également l’objet d’une évaluation croisée qui permettra d’expliciter leur potentiel de réplicabilité dans le pourtour méditerranéen. Cool Noons est un projet innovant pour la manière dont il aborde un sujet aussi urgent que sous-estimé comme l’évolution du tourisme urbain vers un modèle plus durable et pérenne. Il est également innovant pour les outils et méthodes mobilisés qui associent rigueur scientifique, pragmatisme et créativité, et ce grâce à la diversité des partenaires impliqués.

Actual Achievements (EN):
During the first six months of COOL NOONS, the consortium has focused its efforts on setting up the collaborative framework, the key tools to effectively manage and communicate about the project, and the main frameworks to launch the first activities in the pilot cities (Imola, Lisbon, Marseille, Dubrovnik and Budva). For what concerns the project’s management, the lead partner AVITEM has set up several tools. A project Vademecum has been developed with the purpose of summarizing all the key elements related to the project. Several factsheets related to the financial management of the project have also been made available to the partners. An additional document detailing the project management bodies and the rules of procedure of the Steering Committee has also been produced by AVITEM and validated by all partners. A Basecamp space dedicated to COOL NOONS has been set up and is going to be used as the main online repository for the working documents and key productions. A timeline of the COOL NOONS activities has been produced by AVITEM and is used to monitor the progress of all the activities. The consortium has also established a framework to regularly meet and update each other about the progress on the activities. It has been decided that, besides the formal Steering Committee meetings (two per year, one in-person and one online), there will be monthly online meetings every third Tuesday of each month. These meetings are a very effective way to keep each other informed about the progress of the activities and discuss any potential difficulties. A first in-person Steering Committee meeting has been held in Marseille on the 25th March, followed by a training session on Design Thinking on the 26th and 27th March, and a data collection workshop on the 27th March. This meeting has allowed to create a positive collaboration dynamic among the partnership, and to officially kick-off the first activities of the project, and especially the first testing which will take place during summer 2024. The communication of COOL NOONS has also been launched during this first reporting period. The Communication strategy (Deliverable 2.1.1) has been drafted by Imola Faenza Tourism Company (IFTC) with the support of AVITEM and with input from all partners. It has been validated by the JS and is now used as the main framework to carry out the communication activities of the project. The project’s website has been set up and is now online: it acts as our main online showcase space where anyone can find the key information and news about the project. IFTC has also set up the social media channels for the project: Facebook, Instagram and LinkedIn. The project also has a YouTube channel. Partners have also started communicating about COOL NOONS on their own online channels. Following the data collection workshop in Marseille on the 27th March, the University of Coimbra has developed a methodological framework (Del. 1.1.1) to help the pilot partners to collect the data in their territories. The American College of Greece has also drafted a cross-cultural evaluation methodology (Del. 1.1.2) which complements this methodological framework. AVITEM has conducted a training session on Design Thinking and has produced a Design Thinking methodology (Del. 1.1.3), aimed at helping the pilot partners to organise the collective intelligence workshops with local citizens and stakeholders, to co-design solutions to improve the experience of visiting their city (for tourists and residents) during the hottest hours of the day. The municipality of Lisbon has provided instructions and insights to help pilot partners to map their territories, with the aim to identify the Cool Noons paths: itineraries in their cities that connect fresh spots in the city and/or spaces where tourists and residents can find relief from the heat. IFTC has provided instructions and a template for the promotion tools for these paths. Based on this information, and on the testing instructions provided by the Budva municipality, partners are now ready to identify and test their Cool Noons paths. DURA Dubrovnik has provided instructions to partners for the composition of local committees that will follow the project’s activities in the pilot cities. These committees will involve local tourism stakeholders, municipality staff, residents’ representatives, elected officials, technical officers, etc. They will select the ideas generated in the collective intelligence workshops to be implemented in 2025 through the investments. The Metropolitan city of Bologna has shared instructions and tools for the carbon footprint monitoring. AVITEM has started engaging with the horizontal projects, by meeting with the Community4Tourism Angels from the University of Thessaly, attending the C4T meeting in Rome and presenting the project at the Dialogue4Tourism meeting in Marseille. This past year has been marked by significant global challenges—climate disasters, conflicts, and both sorrow and hope across the world. Summer 2024 was the hottest ever recorded on Earth, while Spain endured its worst flooding in decades. These events have underscored the critical need for the tourism sector to adapt to climate change. The Cool Noons project will test solutions for improving urban tourism comfort during the hottest hours of the day, and therefore aims at adapting Mediterranean urban tourism to climate changes. During the first months, the consortium laid the foundation of the project. In April, the project’s kick-off meeting in Marseille established the governance structure and the operational framework of the consortium. 1/ Specific objective: to improve the knowledge of project partners on the behavior/needs of visitors and to expand the range of possible solutions through a co-design process involving citizen Started in January 2024, Cool Noons operates using an innovative methodology that integrates participative and evaluative processes. The University of Coimbra developed a robust methodological framework, instructing pilot cities to gather data across three assessments: 1) weather data, 2) tourist attractiveness, and 3) experience enhancement (for both residents and tourists). ACG-RC contributed by creating a cross-cultural data collection methodology, ensuring the project’s adaptability for future implementation in new cities. In Summer, each pilot city carried out extensive data collection, guided by these frameworks. The methodologies were adapted to the specific context and available tools. For instance, Lisbon’s Municipality and DURA used thermal cameras and QR codes, Marseille’s Municipality conducted surveys via social media, while Budva’s Municipality and Bologna’s Metropolis conducted in-person surveys. Once collected, the data was shared with the University of Coimbra and the American College of Greece as a foundation to refine project strategies and ensure a common analytical approach. One of the key success factors for the Cool Noons project concerns its participative approach. During the project’s kick-off meeting in April, a design thinking workshop was organized. It aimed at expanding the toolkit of each pilot city for a better co-design approach. During Autumn, participatory workshops occurred for most pilot sites with tailor-made methodologies. For example, in Imola, participatory urban walks on the paths were organized with students in Environmental Management, in Accessible and Sustainable Tourism and in Construction, Environment, and Territory. Citizens and NGOs were also involved and the workshops. Another example: Lisbon mixed tools, including participative walks and creation of Lego models to represent a set of selected solutions: afforestation, with native species and those adapted to the ecosystem; soil support and greater carbon sequestration; planting trees along the trail to increase shade; installation of a pergola with a green roof; requalification of the existing information panel; and resurfacing of the parking area to ensure a high level of infiltration. In Marseille, the lead of those citizens workshops was given to the Citizen Participation department, specialized in organizing those participative democratic processes. 2/ Specific objective: To increase the resilience and the attractiveness of urban areas for tourist and inhabitants testing innovative solutions to adapt to climate change Our pilot cities (Lisbon, Marseille, Budva, Dubrovnik, Imola) seek to promote new tourist attractions in less frequented areas and develop solutions to urban heat in the most visited places. In Spring, the pilot cities focused on characterizing tourist points of interest, identifying cool spots (such as green areas and water points) and measuring thermal/weather data. The routes were mapped and further on, they were promoted thanks to digital and printed maps (printed in a few thousand copies for each site) and supported by the Local Tourism Boards. In addition, the co-conceived solutions proposed will contribute to the city’s resilience in the context of climate change and heat waves. In the participatory workshops, priority intervention areas were identified: wind-based solutions, digital-based solutions, water-based solutions, urban furniture-based solutions, shaded-based solutions. The solutions most suited to each location will be implemented during Spring 2025 along the Cool Noons paths. The past few years have been marked by significant global tragedies. Summer 2024 was the hottest ever recorded on Earth, while Spain endured its worst flooding in decades. June 2025 Europe was hit by a quite early heatwave. These events have underscored the critical need for the tourism sector to adapt to climate change, to promote disaster risk prevention, resilience, taking into account eco-system based approaches. During this reporting period, The COOL NOONS project developed in a significant progress, with a strong focus on stakeholder engagement, pilot implementation, and strategic communication. Project coordination Monthly coordination meetings(every third Tuesday afternoon of the month) were held between the project team to ensure smooth implementation of the upcoming activities. A steering committee meeting was host by Municipality of Lisbon, in Portugal, on March 31 and April 1, 2025. The event gathered project partners and associated partners, including representatives from MedCities, Lisbon Tourism Board, Municipality of Dubrovnik and the Dubrovnik’s Maritime Museum. During the meeting, two panels were organized: one about climate change adaptation to heat in each pilot city, the second one focusing on the conditions and perspectives for Mediterranean Sustainable Tourism. Stakeholder engagement Cool Noons operates using an innovative methodology that integrates participative and evaluative processes. Budva organized structured focus groups with key local stakeholders - urban planners, representatives of vulnerable groups, youth organizations, local institutions- in March 2025. In all pilot cities, key achievements include the continuation of visitor feedback along "Cool Paths" to record their experiences regarding heat perception and access to cool spaces. This ongoing data collection and analysis is guided by University of Coimbra & American College of Greece. In Budva, spatial and technical assessments and administrative procedures were completed for greening the corridor between Akademija Znanja and the Old Town. In Imola, the Action Plan for Heat-Resilient Public Spaces shows three cooling routes and twelve key places to adapt with NBS-solutions. It was presented publicly on April 1st, 2025, at the City Council Hall in Imola. In Marseille, 9 pergolas and 33 climbing plants and 3 fountains have been implemented alongside Cool Paths. New Cool Paths are envisioned for the next years. In Dubrovnik, DURA initiated the development of a mobile app featuring augmented reality elements, along with the procurement of tablets and accompanying equipment for the Maritime Museum in Dubrovnik, aiming at promoting the visits of the AC museum. In Lisbon, initial intervention was completed in March 2025. Two additional interventions of NBS are planned in the green corridors and will be noted for the next reporting period. For any investment, a common graphic layout for panels and signs have been designed by IFTC. Replicability of the project MCBO started working on D. 3.2.1 - Guidelines for renewal of public policies, defining the best tools and methods, an action plan and a short list of clear and "ready to use” for decision makers. AVITEM proposed strategic perspectives and reflections: Cool Noons as a methodology and as a program for a renewal of urban planning/fabric. It will be further developed in the Transferring Strategy and Action Plan. Project’s promotion The project was presented in the conference “Surchauffe Urbaine: comment adapter nos centres historiques patrimoniaux?” held on March 10th in Marseille. AVITEM participated in mission’s governance projects and contributes to the amplification strategy of Interreg Euro-Med community. Carbon-footprint monitoring MCBO managed the carbon footprint monitoring, collecting and uploading data to the platform. Partners are strongly committed to limit the production of carbon-footprint.
